The 9 Golden Nutrition Rules

These science-backed principles optimize your metabolism, digestion, and body composition.

1

Only 3 Meals Per Day

Eat exactly three meals per day. No snacking between meals. Research shows this allows insulin levels to drop between meals, promoting fat oxidation and improving insulin sensitivity over time.

2

5-Hour Gap Between Meals

Leave at least 5 hours between each meal. This activates the migrating motor complex (MMC) - your gut's natural cleansing wave - and ensures full digestion before the next meal.

3

Start Each Meal with Protein

Always begin eating with your protein source first. Studies show this stimulates glucagon release, promotes satiety, and reduces the blood sugar spike from carbohydrates eaten afterwards.

4

Only One Type of Protein Per Meal

Each meal should contain only one protein source (e.g., either chicken OR fish OR eggs - never mixed). This simplifies digestion and supports optimal enzymatic breakdown and nutrient absorption.

5

No Eating After 9 PM

Finish your last meal by 9 PM. Chrononutrition research shows that late eating disrupts circadian rhythms, impairs glucose tolerance, and reduces sleep quality and overnight recovery.

6

Drink Plenty of Water

Drink at least 35 ml of water per kg of body weight daily. Hydration supports every metabolic process. Drink between meals rather than during to avoid diluting digestive enzymes.

7

One Apple a Day

Eat one apple daily - ideally in the evening. Apples are rich in pectin, a prebiotic fiber that feeds beneficial gut bacteria, supports detoxification, and helps regulate cholesterol levels.

8

Rotate Your Protein Sources

Don't eat the same protein source twice in one day. Rotating proteins ensures a broad amino acid spectrum and diverse micronutrient intake across the week.

9

Prioritize Sufficient Sleep

Aim for 7-9 hours of quality sleep per night. Sleep deprivation increases ghrelin (hunger hormone) by up to 28% and decreases leptin (satiety hormone), leading to overeating. Poor sleep also raises cortisol, promoting visceral fat storage and impairing muscle recovery. Growth hormone - essential for repair and fat metabolism - is released primarily during deep sleep.
